Golf Ball Distance & Upper Body Biomechanics During a Golf Swing

About

This study investigated the influence of golf ball distance from foot stance on upper body biomechanics during a swing, hypothesizing that beyond 6 inches, elbow, shoulder, and trunk joint Range of Motion (ROM) and angular acceleration decrease, while root mean square (RMS) and peak electromyography (PEMG) muscle activity increase. The objective was to correlate voltage, acceleration, and ROM data with injury risks stemming from improper posture, providing actionable insights for golf athletes.

Task/Action

  • Processed biomechanical data from VISUAL3D and VICON NEXUS, applying MATLAB signal filtration to isolate tissue and ligament stress indicators.
  • Authored comprehensive reports for the Biomedical Engineering head professor, synthesizing upper body muscle group dynamics from multi-axis sensor inputs.
  • Performed quantitative and qualitative analysis of EMG sensor data, mapping muscle activation patterns across swing phases.
  • Constructed 3D models of over 10 muscles and joints via VICON NEXUS, integrating kinematic hinges for real-time motion simulation.
  • Analyzed optimal swing mechanics—biological and mechanical—identifying injury prevention strategies through parametric modeling and experimental validation.

Result

Established an optimal golf ball distance of 6 inches from the stance, validated by MATLAB-calculated RMS and PEMG values, minimizing joint stress and muscle strain. Quantified flexion, extension, and internal angles in X, Y, Z planes, alongside acceleration, velocity, force, and power, using goniometers, EMG sensors, and force plates processed through VISUAL3D and VICON NEXUS. Segmented forearms, upper arms, trunk, and pelvis, with hinged joints modeled for precise biomechanical analysis, offering golfers data-driven posture guidance.

Tech Stack: MATLAB, VISUAL3D, VICON NEXUS, EMG Analysis
